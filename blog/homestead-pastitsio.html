Homestead Pastitsio

Or: what happens when you cook 20 pounds of meat on a Sunday and need to keep a family of seven interested all week

Cooking — April 2026

The Recipe

What You Need (family of 7, 10.5 x 15 glass baking dish)

Pasta: 1 lb mini penne, cooked 2 minutes short in lamb or chicken stock if you have it. Save the pasta water.

Meat sauce: 3 to 3.5 lbs of a beef/pork/lamb sauce, warmed. Thin with the reserved pasta water.

Béchamel: 8 tbsp butter, 8 tbsp flour, 3 cups whole milk, salt, black pepper, pinch of nutmeg. (Make a little extra for that dish size.)

Cheese: Whatever good hard cheese you have — Parmesan, Pecorino, Romano. Quesadilla cheese mixed in for melt. Generous layer over the top.

Finish: Light dusting of ancho chili powder over the cheese. Optional, but do it.

How to Do It

  1. Cook pasta in stock, pull 2 minutes early, save the pasta water, drain.
  2. Warm your sauce, use pasta water to thin it to a good consistency.
  3. Make the béchamel: melt butter, whisk in flour, cook until slightly nutty, whisk in milk slowly until thick, season with salt, pepper, nutmeg.
  4. Mix pasta and sauce together in the baking dish.
  5. Pour béchamel over the top. Don't stir it in. Let it sit.
  6. Cheese over that. Ancho dusting over that.
  7. 350°F, uncovered, 30–35 minutes. Golden on top, bubbling at the edges.
  8. Rest 5–10 minutes before cutting.

The Story

It's baseball season. If you've got kids in little league, you already know what that means for dinner. It means you have roughly 45 minutes between getting home from the field and someone melting down because they're hungry, and you haven't thought about food since 7am. It means you cook big on Sunday or you're surviving on drive-throughs by Wednesday.

So that's what we did. Sunday I fired up the outdoor burner, dropped the big cast iron dutch oven — and when I say big, I mean the kind of pot that makes a standard kitchen fork look like a toothpick — and we made a meat sauce. Not a little meat sauce. Twenty pounds of it. Ground beef, pork loin we ground ourselves, and ground lamb from one of our own sheep. Equal parts, all in.

The kids helped. The whole thing took most of the afternoon and it smelled incredible and that's the kind of Sunday that makes the rest of the week feel manageable.

By Thursday, we'd done spaghetti, penne, and loaded fries covered in meat sauce and cheese. All good. All easy. But the family needed something that felt genuinely different, not just the same sauce on a different shape of pasta.

That's where pastitsio comes in.

What Even Is Pastitsio

It's Greek. It's baked pasta. Most Americans have never heard of it but it tastes like lasagna's more interesting, better-traveled cousin.

The formal version has three layers: tube pasta mixed with egg, a spiced meat sauce with cinnamon and allspice in it, and a thick béchamel on top that bakes into something between a crust and a custard. The warm spices in the sauce are what make it distinctly Greek rather than Italian. If you've ever eaten moussaka, same idea, different carb.

Ours didn't have cinnamon in the sauce because the sauce was already made. But it had lamb, and lamb carries its own version of that warmth. It worked.

The Béchamel Thing

I'd never made béchamel before. Hadn't heard the word a week ago, honestly. Turns out it's just a roux that keeps going — butter and flour cooked together, then you whisk milk into it until it becomes a thick, creamy sauce. That's it. If you've ever made a gravy or a cream sauce, you've already done 80% of this.

The thing I didn't expect: nutmeg. A pinch of it in the béchamel doesn't taste like nutmeg. It tastes like the sauce is more right than it would be without it. One of my kids tasted it straight out of the pan and immediately said "what is that?" He liked it. That's the whole point of nutmeg in a béchamel — it's the thing you can't name.

I used 8 tablespoons of butter, 8 tablespoons of flour, and about 3 cups of milk for a 10.5 x 15 glass baking dish. Next time I'd make slightly more — the dish could've used a little extra coverage — but it came out right.

The Lamb Stock Move

This one wasn't planned, it was just logical. I had lamb stock in the fridge. Instead of boiling the pasta in plain water, I boiled it in the lamb stock. Every piece of pasta absorbed flavor as it hydrated instead of just absorbing hot water. I saved the starchy pasta water from that and used it to thin down the meat sauce while it was warming — keeps everything in the same flavor family, and the starch from the pasta water helps the sauce hold together.

Plain water throws flavor away. If you have stock, use stock.

The Cheese Situation

I had two cheeses from Central Market that I'd been working through: an Artisan Parmesan Cello Riserva and a Pecorino Romano del Lazio, Genuine Fulvi. The Fulvi is actual imported Italian sheep's milk cheese made in the hills outside Rome. I grated both of those over the béchamel along with some of the regular quesadilla cheese for melt factor.

Before it went in the oven I hit the top with a very light dusting of dark ancho chili powder. Barely any — more visual than flavor. That rust-colored dusting over pale cheese against the orange-red sauce visible at the edges. Visual is flavor sometimes. It looked like something.

The Bonus Mac and Cheese

There were extra noodles. The pastitsio was in the oven. Kids were hungry.

I threw the extra noodles back in the pot with just enough water to cover the bottom, medium heat, cubes of butter in to melt, handful of quesadilla cheese and stirred hard, sifted in flour by eye until it looked right, grated in both of the fancy cheeses — threw the whole rind of the Pecorino in there because the rind is concentrated flavor and you don't throw that away — more Parmesan, big scoop of sour cream for body, milk until the consistency felt right.

It was rich. Deeply, heavily rich. Good but one-sided — needed something to cut through it. I hit mine with a Humble House habanero sauce on the plate. The fruity tropical heat of habanero against all that butter and aged sheep cheese. That opened it up.

The lesson: that much fat needs acid built in from the start. Squeeze of lemon or a splash of white wine vinegar before the milk goes in. The hot sauce becomes a bonus instead of the rescue.

The kids ate both dishes. Two pasta dishes on one plate is not a formal dining concept. It works anyway.

Verdict

Wife said the sauce was great. Kids all ate a lot. The cross-section on the cut showed the béchamel layer holding right where it was supposed to. The ancho dusting looked intentional.

The night we actually planned to make this, the kids snuck outside, built a fire in the grill, and roasted hot dogs on sticks without telling anyone. That was Wednesday. The pastitsio happened Thursday. No dishes on Wednesday. Sometimes the family makes the right call.

Make it again: yes. Scale the béchamel up slightly for that size dish. Add cinnamon and allspice to the meat sauce next time if you're starting from scratch — do it right.
